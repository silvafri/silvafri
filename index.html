Social Content & Digital Production










Community Highlights

A large part of my role as a Marketing and Outreach Specialist for Sorority and Fraternity Life is to create, plan, and develop content for our social media. Based on our Fall Strategic Communication plan, I developed content that aligned with our objectives to increase awareness and engagement of our new program.

The portraits above are posted on our Instagram page in a series of posts called “Community Highlights” in which we highlight students of all collegiate backgrounds who are enthusiastic for the development of SFL. On our story, we create extended video content of our student highlights expressing why their peers should get involved in the Spring. So far, these highlights have been our most popular content on the platform.

We use social influence theory in these posts to generate excitement and engagement from our following by showing their fellow peers expressing enthusiasm for the development of SFL.







What is SFL? Video Series

This video is one of a three part video series called “What is CUDenverSFL?” I created the script and worked alongside a student illustrator who created the illustrations for the video.

In our Fall Strategic Communication Plan, we identified that one of our situations was that a large portion of our publics did not know what sorority/fraternity life was. This series was one of the first pieces of content that launched our Instagram last Fall and was strategically placed to introduce and educate our new following on what exactly is a sorority and fraternity life community, how CU Denver’s model strives to lead in its values, and how students can get involved.

Campaign

CU Denver Free Menstrual Products Campaign

Open PDF

Esther Belinsky, a CU Denver student, and I worked on the the CU Denver Free Menstrual Product campaign that recognized the need to establish one of the basic necessities of female, non-binary and transgender health, free menstrual products.

The campaign promoted the health and academic focus of these students by alleviating some of the financial stresses that come with menstruating by providing free menstrual products in CU Denver restrooms.

Our campaign created the research report above as well as a proposal that urged the chancellor to approve the cost of providing free menstrual products as a reoccurring line item in the university’s budget.

Higher administration is developing a ten-year wellness plan to be a leading university in promoting “urban university wellness.” In our documents, we demonstrated the alignment between our campaign and the university’s priority to be a leading school in supporting student health and wellness. After months of lobbying, petitioning, and partnering with stakeholders, we submitted our proposal and managed to secure $14,183 for a pilot program that would dispense free menstrual products in high traffic restrooms in each CU Denver building.

Outreach

Tivoli Tenants Festival Tabling Session

In my role as Marketing and Outreach Specialist, it is efficient to reach our commuter student population through social media but, make equal efforts to establish long lasting impressions through our interpersonal outreach. Interpersonal outreach is a large component of how we establish trust through genuine discussions about sorority and fraternity life and the model we plan on providing for our students. One of our most effective forms of outreach are our tabling sessions around campus. We table biweekly in different high-traffic CU Denver spaces to maximize exposure and engagement.

Before a tabling session, I plan our purpose for tabling and our end goal: get students to sign up on an email list, increase our social media following, provide sorority and fraternity take aways, etc.

The images above are from a tabling session we had during a welcome week festival at school. We wanted to increase our program’s exposure by providing CU Denver SFL SWAG. In order for students to get SWAG, we simply asked them to write on our white board what they thought when they heard “Sorority and Fraternity Life.” In turn, this gave us a general idea of where our publics stood in order to tailor future outreach plans.

After I completed my research, I presented it the VP, the Chief Legal Officer, and members from both the corporate affairs and legal team. Our meeting went over time with the members engaged inquiring about my research and perspective. They provided positive feedback and informed me that my work would be forwarded to leadership. I felt proud of the work I had done.
